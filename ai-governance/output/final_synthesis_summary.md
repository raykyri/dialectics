# AI and Governance: Summary

## The Through-Line

AI governance isn't about building the right tool — it's about initiating practices of collective investigation, where the investigation itself builds shared understanding, trust, and institutional capacity.

## Why AI Hasn't Transformed Governance

The field has been asking "what tool should we build?" when the question is "what practice of collective investigation should we invite communities into?" Additional structural barriers:

- **Market failure:** Governance tools are public goods with no procurement budget. Civic tech funding is orders of magnitude below commercial AI investment.
- **Interaction paradigm gap:** LLMs are built for 1:1; governance is n:n. No major lab has invested in genuinely multi-party AI interaction.
- **Design/deployment confusion:** The field oscillates between "design carefully first" and "deploy now." Both are wrong framings. The right approach is to invite specific communities to collectively investigate specific problems, treating each as an experiment.
- **Tool-centric thinking:** Tools without practices are dashboards. Practices without tools are slow but functional. The practice is what matters.

## The Framework (Three Rounds)

**Round 1 — The Scaffold Thesis:** The question isn't "what should the AI do?" but "what practice should the AI be embedded in?" The practice is the unit of cognition. Outputs of AI-assisted governance are irreducibly hybrid — neither human-generated nor AI-generated.

**Round 2 — The Collective Investigation Thesis:** The epistemic layer and the governance layer are the same activity. Collective investigation IS governance. Communities using AI to jointly research their situations build shared understanding, make the politics of knowledge visible, and create the epistemic common ground governance requires. The fact/value separation is false; investigating entanglement is the work.

**Round 3 — The Invitation Thesis:** The design/deployment binary dissolves. Don't deploy tools — extend invitations. Invite specific communities to collectively investigate shared problems, with AI handling mechanical labor and humans doing judgment work. The distinction that matters is scaling vs. learning, not design vs. deployment.

## What AI IS Good For in Governance

1. **Making collective patterns legible** — clustering opinions, mapping evidence landscapes, surfacing hidden agreements
2. **Helping individuals articulate their own positions** — 1:1 AI that deepens contribution quality while preserving independence
3. **Compressing evidence synthesis** — turning years-long reviews into days-long processes
4. **Making the politics of knowledge visible** — who funded what, which questions were asked, which methodologies chosen
5. **Maintaining institutional memory** — what was decided, why, what happened as a result
6. **Structuring encounter** — generating questions (not answers) that help groups explore their disagreement's structure

## What AI Is NOT Good For (or Needs More Work)

1. **Generating proposals or bridging statements** — the ventriloquism problem remains real
2. **Replacing human judgment on values** — governance always involves irreducible value choices
3. **Operating without trust** — AI governance tools inherit legitimacy from practices, not technical capabilities
4. **Scaling without learning** — premature scaling produces governance theater

## Scope Conditions

AI governance practices work where:
- Power asymmetries aren't so extreme that one party can ignore the process
- There's some institutional surface to embed in (even a shared problem and willing participants)
- The community is both the deployer and the governed (tools FOR communities, not ON communities)
- The deployment treats itself as learning, not scaling

## Most Actionable Projects (Start This Month)

1. **Open Source Contribution Governance** — Structured process for collectively evaluating AI-generated contribution quality. AI surfaces patterns, flags anomalies, maintains shared memory of quality standards.

2. **Participatory Evidence Mapping for Research Communities** — Collaborative evidence map where researchers collectively track what's been replicated, contested, or unknown. AI handles mechanical synthesis; community does judgment.

3. **Research Funding Landscape Maps** — AI-assisted mapping of what's been funded, by whom, with what results. Makes the political economy of research funding legible.

## Longer-Horizon Projects

4. **Climate Coordination Evidence Synthesis** — Jointly investigate what adaptation strategies municipalities have tried. The investigation IS the coordination mechanism.

5. **Platform Worker Collective Intelligence** — Collectively investigate algorithmic systems governing gig workers' livelihoods. Build shared understanding needed for collective action.

6. **Think Tank Transparency** — AI-assisted investigation of the political economy of policy research. Make the politics of knowledge production systematically legible.

7. **AI Safety Research Agenda Governance** — Collectively investigate the structure of disagreement in the field: which parts are factual, which are values, which are framing.

8. **Portable Community Notes** — Community-driven contextualization of contested claims, generalized beyond X as open infrastructure.

9. **Cross-Border Commons Governance** — Collective investigation of shared resource status and governance effectiveness. AI handles monitoring; communities do rule-setting.

10. **Epistemic Governance for Forecasting** — Governance of future-oriented claims in prediction markets and forecasting communities.

## Open Questions (Dialectic Queue)

- Who designs the scaffold? Design authority as unelected governance power
- How does collective investigation handle expert/non-expert asymmetry?
- Can collective investigation scale beyond small groups?
- How does an investigation become an institution?
- What counts as success? How do you evaluate?
- How does AI governance work in power-asymmetric contexts?
- Bad-faith participation in collective investigation
- Governance of AI itself as recursive problem

## The Key Framing Shift

Don't deploy tools. Extend invitations. Invite specific communities to collectively investigate specific problems, with AI handling mechanical labor and humans doing judgment work. The investigation IS the governance. Start immediately, stay small, and refuse the pressure to scale before the practices exist that make scaling meaningful.
