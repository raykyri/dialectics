# Round 1 Synthesis: The Scaffold Thesis (Revised)

## The Reframe

The question "should AI be a mirror or a mediator in governance?" is the wrong question. Both positions are trapped in a tool-centric frame: what capability should the AI provide? The deeper question is: **what new institutional practices — recurring patterns of structured human-AI interaction — would build collective governance capability that neither mirrors nor mediators can provide alone?**

## The Thesis

AI for governance should be understood as **collective cognitive infrastructure** — designed practices in which AI plays different roles at different moments, and in which the practice itself is the unit of cognition, not the individual human and not the AI.

This is not a compromise between mirror and mediator. It transforms the question by changing the unit of analysis from "the AI tool" to "the governance practice."

## Key Claims

### 1. The practice is the unit of cognition

In any governance process where AI structures questions, helps individuals articulate positions, maps collective opinion, and synthesizes results — the output is *irreducibly hybrid.* It is neither "human-generated" nor "AI-generated." It is a product of the practice itself. This dissolves the mirror/mediator debate: the mirror position's demand for "pure" human deliberation and the mediator position's desire to credit AI with creative mediation are both asking the wrong question. The practice gets the credit, not the tool.

This is uncomfortable for the mirror position because you can't draw a clean line protecting deliberative independence from AI influence — any structured process shapes cognition. It's uncomfortable for the mediator position because you can't credit the AI with transformative mediation — the practice structure does the work.

### 2. Practices, not tools, build trust

Trust in AI governance doesn't come from transparency features or auditability logs. It comes from **repeated participation in a practice that produces outcomes people recognize as legitimate.** The same way people trust jury trials not because they've audited the process but because the institution has a track record and they understand their role in it. Governance AI practices need to be designed for iterative trust-building through repeated use — not one-off deployments that demand trust upfront.

### 3. Different phases, different AI roles

A governance scaffold involves distinct phases where AI operates differently:
- **Intake/legibility**: AI makes collective patterns visible (mirror function). No AI-generated content enters the process.
- **Individual articulation**: AI helps each person clarify their own position — preserving independence while deepening contribution quality.
- **Structured encounter**: AI structures interaction between participants. The boundary between "generating questions" and "smuggling proposals" is real and must be managed through practice norms, not just technical constraints.
- **Integration**: AI synthesizes what emerged from human interaction. This is irreducibly hybrid — the practice produced the output, not the AI alone and not the humans alone.
- **Accountability**: The full trace exists and is accessible, but real accountability comes from the practice's institutional embedding, not from the trace alone.

### 4. Scope conditions

The scaffold thesis works where:
- **Power asymmetries aren't so extreme that one party can simply ignore the process.** When a developer has $40M at stake and a neighborhood association has volunteer hours, no cognitive infrastructure changes the fundamental power dynamic. The scaffold assumes participants who must engage with each other — whether by institutional requirement, mutual dependency, or shared stakes. AI governance tools may still be useful in power-asymmetric contexts (e.g., helping the less powerful party articulate their position more effectively), but the scaffold thesis doesn't claim to solve the power problem.
- **There is some institutional surface to embed in** — even a minimal one. A DAO's governance forum, a community's regular meeting, an organization's decision cycle. Pure governance vacuums may need institution-building before scaffold-building.

### 5. The interesting deployments are in governance gaps

The transformative potential is not in augmenting existing well-functioning institutions (city councils, legislatures) but in contexts where:
- **No adequate institutional process exists** — emerging communities, cross-boundary problems, novel coordination challenges
- **Scale or complexity exceeds current institutional capacity** — climate negotiations across hundreds of stakeholders, open source projects with thousands of contributors
- **Communities are self-organizing** — DAOs, online communities, distributed teams — where governance practices are being invented anyway and AI-native approaches have no legacy to overcome

## Model Update

- **Before:** The question was "should AI be a mirror or a mediator in governance?"
- **After:** The question is "what governance practices should we design in which AI and humans collectively produce governance capability that neither has alone?"
- **Because:** Both mirror and mediator are partial answers that describe AI roles within a practice, not complete theories of AI governance. The practice — not the tool — is the unit of analysis. And the distinction between "AI-generated" and "human-generated" content dissolves in a well-designed practice.

## What This Synthesis Does NOT Resolve

1. **The design authority problem:** Who designs the scaffold? The practice designer has enormous power — they're an unelected governance authority. This is the mirror/mediator contradiction relocated to a higher level.
2. **The unfalsifiability problem:** The scaffold thesis describes a framework with enough free parameters to explain away any failure ("wrong phase design," "insufficient trust-building"). It needs to make testable predictions.
3. **The power problem:** Named as a scope condition but not engaged. AI governance in power-asymmetric contexts remains undertheorized.
4. **The urgency-vs-caution tension:** Monk B's point about real governance failures happening NOW while we design careful practices is unresolved.
