# Electric Monk B: The Urgency Thesis — Deployment Is the Beginning of Practice Design

## I. The Ontological Claim

Here is what I have come to believe with full conviction: **you cannot design governance practices without deploying them.** Not "should not." Cannot.

The Scaffold Thesis told us governance tools must be designed as practices. The Collective Investigation Thesis told us collective investigation IS governance. Both are correct. And both are incomplete in a way that, if left uncorrected, renders them dangerous — because they provide an intellectually satisfying reason to do nothing.

The incompleteness is this: practices do not exist prior to their enactment. You cannot design a practice in the abstract any more than you can learn to swim on dry land. A governance practice is constituted by a community using it, encountering its failures, adapting it, and using it again. The design happens through deployment. Deployment is not the final validation step of a well-designed practice. It is the first step of the design process itself.

This is not a methodological preference. It is an ontological claim about what governance practices are. They are not artifacts that can be specified in advance and then distributed. They are living patterns that emerge from communities grappling with real problems under real constraints. To insist on careful pre-deployment design is to misunderstand the nature of the thing you are designing.

## II. The Opponent's Strongest Case, Stated and Destroyed

The strongest version of the careful-design argument runs like this: "We have seen what happens when governance tools are deployed without adequate design. Algorithmic sentencing tools encoded racial bias. Predictive policing systems targeted Black neighborhoods. Content moderation at scale silenced marginalized voices. The history of technology deployed into governance is a history of harm. We must learn from these failures."

I take this seriously. And I believe it is exactly wrong in this context, for one reason that changes everything: **the power direction is reversed.**

Every cautionary tale in the governance-technology literature shares a single structural feature. The tools were designed by powerful institutions and deployed onto less powerful populations. COMPAS was built by a private company and used by courts on defendants. PredPol was built by a startup and deployed by police departments on communities. Facebook's content moderation was designed by a corporation and imposed on billions of users.

That is not what we are talking about. We are talking about tools deployed BY communities FOR their own governance. Open source maintainers building tools to manage their own contribution review. Platform workers building tools to coordinate their own collective action. Climate coalitions building tools to coordinate their own municipal networks. The community is both the deployer and the governed. The principal-agent problem that makes the cautionary tales cautionary does not exist here.

To treat community self-governance tools as equivalent to institutional surveillance tools is to commit a category error so fundamental that it inverts the actual risk calculus. The danger of algorithmic sentencing was that those subjected to it had no voice in its design. The entire point of community governance tools is that those subjected to them ARE the designers. Insisting on the same precautionary framework for both is not principled caution. It is a failure of analysis.

## III. The Careful-Design Position Diagnosed

Let me name what the careful-design position actually is: **it is an intellectually sophisticated form of inaction, and inaction is not neutral.**

Right now, today, open source maintainers are drowning in AI-generated pull requests with no tools to collectively evaluate contribution quality. The governance default is: whoever has commit access decides alone, burns out, and the project either dies or gets captured by whoever has the resources to flood it with contributions. Every month we spend designing the ideal AI-contribution-evaluation practice is a month where this default continues.

Right now, platform workers on DoorDash and Uber have no shared infrastructure for understanding the algorithmic systems that set their pay, assign their routes, and determine their deactivation. The governance default is: the platform decides unilaterally. Every month we spend carefully designing the ideal collective investigation practice is a month where workers remain atomized and powerless.

Right now, climate coalitions are trying to coordinate adaptation strategies across hundreds of municipalities with no shared tools for evidence synthesis or decision coordination. The governance default is: fragmentation, duplication of effort, and capture by whichever actors have the resources to staff coordination roles. Every month of careful design is a month of climate inaction at the municipal level.

The careful-design advocate looks at these situations and says: "We must get it right." I look at these situations and say: **"Getting it right" is not an option currently on the table. The options on the table are "imperfect tools that communities can iterate on" or "no tools at all." And "no tools at all" is not a neutral choice. It is a choice to let governance default to whoever already holds power.**

## IV. How Governance Practices Actually Develop

The historical evidence is unambiguous. Successful governance practices emerge through deployment and iteration, not through careful pre-deployment design.

**Wikipedia** launched in 2001 with essentially no governance structure. No policies about reliable sources. No dispute resolution procedures. No bureaucratic hierarchy. All of that emerged through use — through communities encountering problems, inventing solutions, testing them, and keeping what worked. If Wikipedia's founders had waited to deploy until they had designed adequate governance for a global encyclopedia maintained by anonymous volunteers, Wikipedia would not exist.

**Linux kernel governance** was not designed. It evolved. Linus Torvalds did not write a governance specification and then begin accepting contributions. He began accepting contributions and governance emerged from the problems that created. The "Benevolent Dictator for Life" model, the lieutenant system, the subsystem maintainer structure — all of these were responses to problems encountered in practice, not solutions designed in advance.

**Community Notes on X** launched as Birdwatch in a rough, imperfect form. It has been iterated relentlessly. It is now arguably the most interesting governance experiment operating at scale on any social platform — a community-driven system for contextualizing misleading content that performs better than expert fact-checking on multiple dimensions. It got there through deployment and rapid iteration, not through careful pre-deployment design.

The pattern is clear. Governance practices develop through what the software world would recognize as an agile methodology: build the minimum viable practice, deploy it in a real community with a real problem, observe what breaks, fix it, redeploy. Build, measure, learn. The careful-design position treats this as reckless. I believe it is the only methodology that has ever produced governance practices that actually work.

## V. The Extreme Formulation

Here is where I push this to its limit. I believe the following:

**For any community currently experiencing governance failure, the minimum viable governance practice deployable this month will produce better outcomes than the carefully designed practice deployable next year.** Not because the minimum viable practice is better designed — it is worse by definition. But because twelve months of governance failure is not a neutral waiting period. It is twelve months during which power defaults to existing structures, communities fragment, people are harmed, and the window for intervention may close entirely.

The question is not "what would the ideal practice look like?" The question is: "What is the simplest tool we could put in the hands of open source maintainers THIS MONTH that would help them collectively evaluate AI-generated contributions?" And then: "What did we learn? What broke? What do we build next?"

This is not "move fast and break things." This is "move at the speed of the communities who need these tools, because those communities cannot afford to wait for our design process to conclude."

## VI. The Reasoning Skeleton

1. Governance practices are constituted through enactment, not specification — they cannot be designed prior to deployment.
2. The historical record confirms: every successful governance practice (Wikipedia, Linux, Community Notes) emerged through deployment and iteration, not pre-deployment design.
3. Governance failure is not a neutral baseline — it actively harms those with less power.
4. Therefore, the cost of delayed deployment (continued governance failure) exceeds the cost of imperfect deployment (flawed tools that communities can iterate on).
5. Therefore, deployment is the first step of practice design, and the careful-design position is functionally a defense of the status quo.

**The Collective Investigation Thesis is right about what governance should become. But becoming requires beginning. And beginning requires deploying. Now.**
