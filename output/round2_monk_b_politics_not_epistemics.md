# Electric Monk B: The Political Nature of Governance Cannot Be Epistemized Away

## I. What Governance Actually Is

Let me begin with an ontological claim that the epistemic infrastructure position fundamentally misunderstands.

Governance is not the process by which a community discovers the right answer and implements it. Governance is the process by which communities with genuinely incompatible values, interests, and visions of the good negotiate coexistence without destroying each other. Evidence is one input to that negotiation. It is never the decisive input, and it cannot be, because the negotiation is not about what is true. It is about what matters. About who bears costs. About which future we are building and who gets to decide.

The proposal to ground AI governance tools in epistemic infrastructure — better evidence synthesis, faster systematic reviews, AI-native think tanks, improved peer review — mistakes a symptom for a cause. The governance failures we face are not failures of knowledge. They are failures of politics. And politics cannot be fixed with better epistemics, because politics is not broken epistemics. Politics is a different thing entirely.

## II. The Opponent's Strongest Case, In Their Own Terms

The best version of the epistemic infrastructure argument goes something like this: "We agree governance involves values. But value disputes play out on factual terrain. People who disagree about climate policy disagree partly about climate science. People who disagree about AI regulation disagree partly about AI capabilities. If we could at least get the factual substrate right — if everyone were working from the same evidence base — the value disputes would become clearer, more honest, more tractable. We're not replacing politics with epistemics. We're giving politics better inputs."

This is sophisticated. It is also wrong, and wrong in a way that reveals the deeper problem.

Consider COVID. The epistemic infrastructure advocate says: faster evidence synthesis would have helped. If we had known sooner which interventions worked, governance would have been better. But this profoundly misreads what happened. The evidence on masks, distancing, and vaccines arrived with remarkable speed — historically unprecedented speed. The governance failure was not that evidence was slow. It was that different communities had different risk tolerances, different valuations of individual liberty versus collective safety, different trust relationships with the institutions producing evidence, and different baseline beliefs about the role of the state. Faster evidence synthesis would have produced faster disagreement about what the evidence *means*, because meaning is not contained in the evidence. Meaning is constructed through the political and cultural frameworks people bring to evidence.

The factual disputes in COVID were largely *downstream* of value disputes, not upstream. People didn't distrust vaccines because they lacked evidence. They distrusted vaccines because they distrusted the institutions producing the evidence, and that distrust was itself grounded in real experiences of institutional failure, condescension, and exclusion. You cannot fix a trust problem with more evidence from the distrusted institution. That is not how trust works.

## III. The Specific Failure of Epistemic Infrastructure

The epistemic infrastructure position fails at the precise point where it would matter most. Look at AI safety research governance — the domain where, if anywhere, better evidence synthesis should help, because the community is small, technical, and ostensibly committed to truth-seeking.

The field cannot agree on priorities. Not because evidence is lacking but because researchers hold fundamentally different values. The existential risk camp and the near-term harms camp are not arguing about facts. They are arguing about what matters. Is a speculative but potentially civilisation-ending risk more important than documented, ongoing harms to marginalised communities? That is not a factual question. No amount of evidence synthesis will resolve it, because it is a question about moral weight, about whose suffering counts, about time horizons and discount rates and what kind of future we owe to whom.

Open versus closed development — same structure. The disagreement is not about whether open models enable misuse (everyone agrees they somewhat do) or whether closed models concentrate power (everyone agrees they somewhat do). The disagreement is about which risk is more tolerable, and that is a value judgment rooted in different visions of the relationship between individuals, corporations, and the state.

Now extend this to think tanks and policy research. The proposal for AI-native think tanks imagines that the problem with existing think tanks is insufficient analytical capacity. But the actual problem with think tanks is *capture* — funding structures determine which questions get asked and which answers get amplified. The Heritage Foundation and the Brookings Institution do not disagree because one has better evidence than the other. They disagree because they serve different constituencies with different values and interests. An AI-native think tank with superior synthesis capabilities would simply be a more efficient tool for whoever funds it. The political economy of knowledge production is the bottleneck, not the epistemics.

Research funding governance makes the point from another angle. ARIA exists because someone made a political decision that frontier research in certain domains matters. That decision — which questions deserve resources — is irreducibly political. AI can optimise within a priority framework, but it cannot generate the framework, because the framework is a statement of values. Should we fund fusion research or malaria nets? That is not an evidence question. Both options have evidence bases. The choice between them is about what kind of world you want to build.

## IV. Knowledge and Power

There is a deeper principle here that the epistemic infrastructure position occludes, and this is where I must be direct rather than diplomatic.

The governance of evidence — who produces it, who synthesizes it, who decides what questions are worth investigating, whose evidence counts as evidence — is itself a political process. And it is political all the way down. You cannot resolve the politics of evidence production with better evidence, because you would need a meta-evidence process to adjudicate, and that process would have its own politics. It is turtles all the way down. The epistemic layer has governance problems that are political, not epistemic, and no amount of recursion resolves this.

This is not a novel observation. Science and Technology Studies has documented for decades how "objective evidence" is produced through social processes embedded in power structures. The "evidence-based policy" movement — which the epistemic infrastructure position inherits — is itself a case study in how epistemic framing advantages certain political positions while appearing neutral. When you declare that policy should be "evidence-based," you are making a political claim about whose knowledge counts, which methodologies are legitimate, and what kinds of questions deserve answers. You are advantaging those whose values happen to align with what randomised controlled trials can measure and disadvantaging those whose concerns are structural, historical, or moral.

## V. The Extreme Implication

Push this to its limit: the fantasy of epistemic infrastructure as governance foundation is the latest iteration of a centuries-old technocratic dream. If we could just get everyone to agree on the facts, governance would be easy. Plato's philosopher-kings. Comte's positivist priesthood. The mid-century systems theorists. The evidence-based policy movement. And now: AI-enhanced epistemic infrastructure. Each iteration is more sophisticated than the last, and each fails for the same reason. Governance is hard not because people are ignorant but because they genuinely, irreducibly, legitimately disagree about what matters. And a free society must allow them to.

## VI. The Load-Bearing Claim

Here is the reasoning skeleton. Governance disputes that matter are primarily value disputes with factual dimensions, not factual disputes with value dimensions. Epistemic infrastructure addresses the factual dimensions. Therefore, epistemic infrastructure addresses the secondary rather than the primary feature of governance disputes. Therefore, treating epistemic infrastructure as foundational is a category error — one that systematically advantages those whose political positions are legible to epistemic frameworks while marginalising those whose positions are grounded in values, experience, or identity that resist evidentiary framing.

The load-bearing claim: **the distinction between "factual dimension" and "value dimension" in governance disputes is itself a political act of framing, and whoever controls that framing controls the outcome.** Epistemic infrastructure does not transcend this problem. It *is* this problem.
