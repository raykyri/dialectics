# Round 2 Synthesis: The Collective Investigation Thesis

## The Reframe

The question "should AI build epistemic infrastructure for governance, or is governance irreducibly political?" dissolves when you recognize that **collective investigation IS governance.** The process by which a community jointly researches its own situation — what's happening, why, what the evidence says, whose evidence it is, what questions aren't being asked — is not preparation for governance. It is governance.

## The Thesis

AI's most natural governance role is as **infrastructure for collective inquiry** — supporting communities in jointly investigating the questions that matter to them, where the investigation itself builds the shared understanding, institutional trust, and epistemic common ground that governance requires.

This dissolves the fact/value separation. When communities do epistemic work together — with AI handling mechanical labor and making the structure of disagreement legible — the "epistemic layer" and the "governance layer" are the same activity.

## Key Claims

### 1. Collective investigation IS governance
The process of jointly researching a situation builds the shared understanding, trust, and institutional capacity that governance requires. Investigation and governance are not sequential layers — they are the same activity.

### 2. The fact/value entanglement is a feature, not a problem
Both Round 2 monks assumed facts and values are separable. They aren't. What counts as a "fact" depends on what you value; what you value is informed by what you believe is possible. Collective investigation explores this entanglement as a single object of shared inquiry rather than trying to sort things into bins.

### 3. Making the politics of knowledge visible IS the epistemic infrastructure worth building
Monk B was right that knowledge production is political. Monk A was right that AI can make things legible. Combined: AI's distinctive role is making the politics of knowledge production visible — who funded what, which questions were asked, which methodologies were chosen, who benefits from which framing. This operationalizes the political critique through epistemic tools.

## Concrete Applications

- **Collective evidence mapping:** AI helps a community collectively map what's known, what's contested, what's unknown, and what's being asked by whom — including the politics of who's asking.
- **Structured disagreement investigation:** AI helps communities map the structure of their disagreement — which parts are factual, which are about values, which are about framing.
- **Participatory evidence synthesis:** Communities participate in evaluating evidence with AI handling mechanical labor, building epistemic capacity and trust simultaneously.
- **Transparent question-governance:** Making the decision about which questions get investigated into a visible, participatory governance practice.

## Scope Conditions (from Round 1)
- Power asymmetries can't be so extreme that one party ignores the process
- Some institutional surface needed (even minimal)
- Most transformative in governance gaps where practices are being invented

## Unresolved Tensions
1. The expertise problem: How does collective investigation handle expert/non-expert asymmetry?
2. The scale problem: Can collective investigation scale beyond small groups?
3. The adversarial problem: Bad-faith participation in collective investigation
